In this study, 10 classical and representative machine learning algorithms were employed to develop survival prediction models, including CoxBoost (boosting in Cox regression), Lasso (least absolute shrinkage and selection operator), Ridge, plsRcox (partial least squares regression for Cox), RSF (random survival forest), SuperPC (supervised principal components), GBM (generalized boosted regression modeling), stepwise Cox, Enet (elastic network), and survival-SVM (survival support vector machine). These algorithms characterize variable–outcome relationships and risk structures in survival data from different perspectives, integrating both linear modeling and nonlinear learning capabilities. Accordingly, they offer complementary strengths in dimensionality reduction for high-dimensional data, variable selection, coefficient shrinkage, capture of complex interactions, and robust prediction. Among them, Lasso, RSF, stepwise Cox, and CoxBoost inherently incorporate feature selection procedures, enabling the identification of the most prognostically relevant variables during model construction while reducing interference from redundant information and noisy features, thereby improving model interpretability and generalizability.
